Shift Cipher

The Shift Cipher is one of the earliest forms of encryption, where each letter in the alphabet is shifted by a fixed number of positions. When the shift value is three, it is often referred to as the Caesar Cipher.

The method involves assigning the letter A the number 0, the letter B the number 1, and so on, until Z is assigned the number 25. The plaintext message is then converted into a sequence of numbers. To acquire the ciphertext, the secret key k modulo 26 is added to each number, and the resulting sequence of numbers is then translated back into letters.

Since there are only 26 possible keys, the shift cipher can be easily broken through brute force. 

For example:

Shift → 3

Plaintext → Who in the world am I? Ah, that’s the great puzzle.

If the ciphertext you are decrypting is big enough you can actually calculate the ciphertext letter frequencies yourself! But this would require a really long message.

For this example, you are given the letter frequency for a ciphertext letter. So if you know that in the ciphertext a letter has the frequency 12.7 and that for English letter frequencies the letter E has the frequeny 12.7 then you can calculate shift that way. However some letters have the same frequency, for example, both the letters C and U have the frequency 2.8. If you know a ciphertext letter has this frquency, it may take some trial and error to determine the correct shift. However once you have calculated your two potential shift keys, only one will translate your ciphertext into something that makes sense.

For this example, lets say the letter A in the ciphertext has the frequency 2.8. This means that A in ciphertext is either C or U in plaintext. Using this knowledge you can calculate that your shift key will either be 6 or 24.

Let's check a shift of 6 first:
Ciphertext → G ayl'r em zyai rm wcqrcpbyw zcaysqc G uyq y bgddcpclr ncpqml rfcl.
Would give us a plaintext of:
Plaintext → A usf'l yg tsuc lg qwklwjvsq twusmkw A osk s vaxxwjwfl hwjkgf lzwf.

However this doesn't make much sense, so lets check a shift of 24. This gives us the following plaintext which makes a lot more sense.
Ciphertext → G ayl'r em zyai rm wcqrcpbyw zcaysqc G uyq y bgddcpclr ncpqml rfcl.
Plaintext → I can't go back to yesterday because I was a different person then.

If you know the ciphertext's letter frequencies these can be used to work out the shift key, or, depending on the size of the text you are decrypting you are able to calculate frequencies yourself.
